Mysore Pak

Mysore Pak originated in the royal kitchen of the Mysore Palace in Karnataka, India, during the reign of King Krishnaraja Wadiyar IV. The word “Pak” means sweet concoction in Sanskrit, so "Mysore Pak" roughly means “sweet from Mysore.” Mysore Pak has a long shelf life, especially the traditional version, which can stay fresh for several days without refrigeration.

Payasam

Payasam is a traditional South Indian dessert often served during festivals, weddings, and temple offerings. It symbolizes celebration and prosperity. In many temples, payasam is offered as prasadam (holy food), especially in Kerala and Tamil Nadu. Though it’s usually the last dish in a meal, payasam is often the most awaited part—offering a sweet finish to a festive South Indian feast.

Palakova

Palakova is a traditional Indian sweet made mainly from milk and sugar. This sweet is especially popular in South India, particularly in Tamil Nadu. It is often prepared and served during festivals, weddings, and celebrations. Palakova is high in calcium and energy, since it’s made by reducing milk over time.

Laddoos

Laddoos are popular Indian sweets enjoyed during festivals, celebrations, and special occasions. Laddoos are usually round, small ball-shaped sweets that are easy to hold and eat. They often include ghee, nuts, cardamom, and sugar, making them flavorful and aromatic. Laddoos are offered in temples, shared in homes, and used in religious rituals and ceremonies.

Jilebi

Jilebi is a favorite dessert in India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and other South Asian countries, often enjoyed during festivals and celebrations. It has a signature spiral or pretzel-like shape, which makes it stand out from most other sweets. It has a signature spiral or pretzel-like shape, which makes it stand out from most other sweets. While called "Jilebi" in the south, it's known as "Jalebi" in the north of India, and has variations like Zlabia in the Middle East and North Africa.
